Congratulations for diving into the world of analog synthesizers! Creating sounds on a physical piece of hardware instead of computer software can be challenging at first, but crafting the sound with physical knobs instead of a mouse provides a much more intimate connection with your sound design.

Today, we will be programming a simple but powerful bass sound that can be used in many different styles of music. After this tutorial, you should be comfortable with navigating around the MS-20 Mini, and be able to play around and make custom sounds on your own.

Throughout this tutorial are audio files you can play to compare your sound to what it should roughly sound like.

You will need:
* Korg MS-20 Mini

### *Sidenote: What does "Patch" mean?*

The word "patch" can be used as a verb or noun in synth lingo.

As a verb: "*to patch*" means to connect two ports of a synth together via a cable. Most modern synthesizers (such as the MS-20 mini) use 3.5mm audio cables, which are commonly used as "AUX cords". Patching together combinations of inputs and outputs gives analog synths to power to create diverse sounds.

As a noun: a synth *patch* is a configuration that makes a certain sound. A *bass patch* would make a low sound that sounds like a bass, while a *bell patch* would sound more like a high pitched bell.

So in a sentence: *patching* together different ports can make different sounding synth *patches*.

## Step 1: Turn On the MS-20 Mini

The MS-20 Mini's volume knob also serves as the power switch. Turn the knob clockwise until you hear a click, you should then see a red light labeled "On" turn on.

## Step 2: Tune The Synth

*Due to the analog nature of the MS-20, before starting you should check if the synth is in tune. A variety of factors such as heat and humidity can cause the internal circuits to change slowly over time and fall out of tune. This is normal, so it's good to re-tune your synth every so often.*

Grab your tuning device. If you have a smartphone, there are many tuning apps available.

Hold down the C key on the keyboard. Compare the pitch to what it says on the tuning device.

Locate the **Master Tune** knob on the MS-20. It is on the left-bottom side, next to the **Portamento** knob. Continue to hold the C key while adjusting the the Master Tune knob up or down until the pitches match.

## **Creating the Basic Sound**

***

## Step 3: Configuring VCO1


Locate the section headed **Voltage Controlled Oscillator 1** (*VCO1*). It is the leftmost panel on the upper half of the MS-20.

The highest knob is labeled **Wave Form**. This knob controls the basic sound that this oscillator will produce. Turn this knob to the 1st position, which looks like a square wave.

Below the *Wave Form* knob is a knob labeled **PW**. *PW* stands for *Pulse Width*, which is how wide or narrow the square wave is. Turn this knob all the way to the left.

Below the *Wave Form* knob is the knob labeled **Scale**. This changes the octave of the waveform. Turn this to the lowest setting labeled __*32'*__, which all the way on the left.

## Step 4: Configuring VCO2


Locate the section labeled **Voltage Controlled Oscillator 2** (*VCO2*), directly to the right of section *VCO1*. 

Locate the **Wave Form** knob, similar to Step 3. Turn the knob to the 1st position, which looks like a sawtooth wave.

Locate the **Scale** knob for VCO2(similar to VCO1). Turn this to the lowest position labeled *__16'__*, all the way on the left.

## Step 5: Configuring the Lowpass Filter

*Currently, the sound has a lot of high frequencies. Cutting these out will give a nicer bass sound. The __Lowpass Filter__ filters out the high frequencies and only leaves (passes through) the lower ones.*

Locate the section labeled "Voltage Controlled Lowpass Filter" (*VCF*). Focus on the large knob that says **Cutoff Frequency**. Turn the *Cutoff Frequency* knob to position 4.

Locate the smaller **Peak** knob under the *Cutoff Frequency* knob. Turn the *Peak* knob to position 5.

## **Volume Envelope**

A volume envelope has 4 main components:


* **Attack**
   * How long the sound takes to go from silence to the maximum volume.
* __Decay__
   * How long it takes the sound to go from the maximum volume to the sustain volume
* __Sustain__
   * The volume that the sound stays at for the duration of the held note
* __Release__
   * How long it takes for the sound to fade out after the key is released

These are often abbriviated as *ADSR*.
